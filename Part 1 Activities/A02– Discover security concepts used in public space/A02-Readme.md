# A02 – Discover security concepts used in public space

One public space where I clearly observed several security concepts working together was the Perth Busport, which I use often as a student when travelling across the city. What stood out most was that the space is built around controlled movement and active monitoring, not just open public access. I noticed CCTV cameras positioned near the gates and waiting areas, and this matches Transperth’s wider network security model, which includes hundreds of CCTV cameras, Transit Officers, and a Central Monitoring Room that continuously observes activity across stations and platforms. I also regularly saw security officers patrolling the Busport, which adds a visible deterrent and makes response faster if something suspicious happens. A more interesting concept was the way public and operational areas are separated. The automated glass doors near the bus bays are not accessible to ordinary passengers, and staff or drivers use ID-based entry to move through those areas. This shows that even in a public transport space, access is still limited according to role and function rather than being equal for everyone.

Another public place where I observed a different kind of security design was Kmart, especially at the self-checkout area. Here, the main security concepts were not gates or patrols, but transaction validation, discreet monitoring, and exit verification. I noticed that after scanning an item, the checkout would not properly let the process continue unless the item was placed on the platform beside it. This suggests that the system is not only reading the barcode but also checking that the physical action matches the digital purchase record, which is a strong example of an automated security control in a retail public space. I also noticed a small camera above each counter directly facing the customer and checkout area, creating another layer of monitoring that many people may not immediately notice. On top of that, staff at the exit often scan receipts before customers leave, so there is still a final human verification step after the automated system has done its checks. Together, this showed me that retail security can combine digital validation, camera-based oversight, and staff review to reduce theft and suspicious behaviour. Recent findings by Australia’s Privacy Commissioner also show that Kmart used facial recognition technology in parts of its business to tackle refund fraud, which highlights how advanced digital monitoring can now be part of public retail security as well.
