# A10 – Discover privacy technique used offline


One offline privacy technique I discovered is Face ID / Touch ID with Secure Enclave-based biometric storage. I found this especially relevant because it protects the privacy of my phone and my data by placing a strong lock on the device itself, without needing the internet to do its main job. Apple explains that when Face ID or Touch ID is set up, the biometric information is processed, encrypted, and stored inside the Secure Enclave, which is a dedicated security component inside the device. This means the biometric data is not handled like an ordinary photo or file, and it stays tied to the physical device. I also found this useful in everyday life because if I hand my phone to someone to show them one thing, I still do not want them going through my chats or opening other apps that contain very private information. That is why using biometric locks on the phone, and also on specific apps, is such a strong privacy technique: it helps keep sensitive personal data protected even when the device is physically in someone else’s hands.

In simple terms, the privacy technique works by turning the fingerprint or face into a mathematical representation and storing it securely inside the Secure Enclave. Later, when the device is unlocked, the new scan is compared against the stored template inside the device itself, rather than sending that biometric data elsewhere. Apple also explains that the biometric sensor and the Secure Enclave have a strict separation of responsibilities and a secure connection between them. What makes this such a strong offline privacy example is that it protects access to the device and its sensitive apps locally, right at the point where privacy could be violated, without depending on an online service to make the decision.
