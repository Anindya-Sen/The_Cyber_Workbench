# A15 – Discover 5 recent security incidents


One recent security incident I found especially serious was the Marks & Spencer (M&S) cyberattack in 2025. What made this incident stand out was that it caused both major business disruption and customer-data exposure. Public reporting showed that M&S had to suspend online clothing and home orders and deal with ongoing disruption across parts of its operations, while later updates confirmed that attackers had also taken some personal customer information. M&S said the stolen data could include contact details, dates of birth, and online order history, although it did not include usable payment-card details or account passwords. Reports about the attack also said the intrusion was linked to social engineering and a third-party contractor pathway, meaning the attackers did not simply smash through a technical wall, but instead gained access by exploiting people and support processes. That makes this incident more than just a normal data breach — it appears to have involved a mix of human manipulation, third-party risk, and large-scale operational disruption. Another major point is the financial impact: widely accessible reporting said M&S expected the attack to cost around £300 million, which shows how expensive a cyberattack can become even after the initial access has already happened. I think this is a very strong example of a recent security incident because it shows how one attack can affect services, customer trust, internal operations, and company finances all at once, and how recovery can take a long time rather than being fixed immediately.

Another recent incident that stood out to me was the Coinbase breach in 2025, because it was very different from the usual image of hackers breaking through a firewall. Coinbase said cybercriminals bribed and recruited rogue overseas support agents to abuse their legitimate access to customer-support systems and steal data relating to a small subset of customers. According to Coinbase, the stolen information was then used to support social engineering attacks and an extortion demand. The company stressed that passwords, private keys, and customer funds were not directly exposed, which is important because it shows the incident was serious without being a total collapse of its crypto-custody systems. Even so, the breach was dangerous because the attackers were able to obtain customer account data that could help them impersonate Coinbase and trick people into transferring money. Coinbase said it would reimburse customers who were deceived into sending funds and that it refused to pay the attackers’ ransom demand. What makes this incident especially useful is that it shows a real insider-assisted data breach, where the core weakness was not only a technical flaw but the abuse of trusted internal access. It is a strong reminder that security incidents can come from compromised people and processes, not only from malware or coding vulnerabilities.

Another recent security incident I found was the adidas customer-data breach in 2025, and what makes it especially useful for this task is that it shows how a company can still suffer a serious incident even when the attackers do not directly break into the main website first. Public reporting and adidas’s own notice said that an unauthorised external party accessed customer information through a third-party customer service provider, which makes this a strong example of a third-party or supply-chain style breach. The exposed information mainly involved contact details of customers who had previously contacted adidas customer service, and reports said that passwords, payment card data, and other highly sensitive financial information were not affected. Even though that sounds less severe than a full payment-card breach, I still think it is a strong recent incident because customer contact information can still be used for phishing, impersonation, spam, and targeted scams. What also makes this case important is that it shows how security does not stop at the main company’s own systems. If a connected vendor or support provider is weaker, that becomes a real path for attackers. adidas said it moved quickly to contain the incident, launched an investigation with information security experts, and began notifying affected customers. I found this incident useful because it clearly shows how modern cyber incidents often involve vendor risk, data exposure, and customer trust damage, even when the organisation itself was not directly “hacked” in the most obvious way.

One recent security incident that stood out to me was the MedImpact ransomware incident in October 2025. MedImpact publicly confirmed that it had identified ransomware on certain systems and immediately started containment and mitigation measures, while outside reporting added more detail by linking the incident to the Qilin ransomware group. Cybernews reported that Qilin claimed responsibility and said it had exfiltrated around 160 GB of data, including samples that appeared to contain financial operations records, commission reports, bank summaries, and claims-related business data. Additional reporting described MedImpact as serving more than 50 million healthcare members, which makes the incident more significant because pharmacy benefit managers sit at the centre of a large healthcare ecosystem connecting patients, insurers, pharmacies, and payers. MedImpact said it brought in a leading cybersecurity firm, notified authorities, and began rebuilding affected systems in a more segregated environment, while outside reporting noted that core pharmacy claims processing had started coming back online during recovery. What I think makes this a strong case is that it combines several major cybersecurity themes at once: a confirmed ransomware attack, potential data exfiltration, likely extortion pressure, and serious operational risk in a healthcare-related service provider. Even though the company did not publicly verify every claim made by the ransomware group, the broader coverage helps show the likely scale and seriousness of the incident much more clearly than the company statement alone.

Another recent incident I found very detailed was the Crunchyroll breach reported in March 2026. TechRadar reported that the attacker allegedly infected a support agent’s computer at an outsourced provider, accessed the agent’s Okta single sign-on account, and then used that foothold to reach Crunchyroll’s Zendesk support environment and other internal systems. The report said the attacker may have exfiltrated up to 8 million support tickets covering around 6.8 million users, including usernames, emails, IP addresses, geographic information, and ticket contents. Payment information was reportedly not affected unless a user had entered it into a support ticket. There were also claims of a $5 million ransom demand, while Crunchyroll said it was investigating and there was no indication of continued access. What I think makes this incident very strong is that it combines several different security themes: outsourced support risk, identity compromise, administrative platform access, possible extortion, and a very large amount of user-related information. It is a good example of how attackers can do major damage without necessarily breaching the main customer-facing product directly.
