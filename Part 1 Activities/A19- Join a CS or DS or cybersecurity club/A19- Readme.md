## A19- Join a CS/DS/cybersecurity club.


For this activity, I joined the UCC (University Computer Club) at UWA, which was actually one of the clubs I had been most interested in for a long time. I was unable to join earlier because of some personal reasons, so I had to wait until my second year to finally become a member. When I went through the joining process, I ran into a small bit of confusion on the membership page. One option said “first time members only” while another said “student and UWA guild member.” At first, the wording made it sound like the first option could apply to anyone joining the club for the first time, so I was unsure which one to choose.

After I contacted the club president, Ari Carter, she explained that the “first time members only” option was actually meant for freshers who were starting university in that semester, which made much more sense afterwards. I also noticed a small issue in the form during registration, and after I raised it, it was fixed quickly, which left me with a good impression because it showed that the club was responsive and willing to help members when something was unclear. That part of the experience made the joining process feel much more welcoming, because I did not just register and leave, but actually had some interaction with the club leadership as well.

After joining, I got a much better idea of what the club offers and why it is valuable. I was introduced to some of the practical benefits of the club space, including the high-speed internet and the supportive, project-focused environment. One thing that stood out to me was that they often organise project nights, where students can discuss university assignments or work on personal projects with help from others. I found this especially exciting because in technical subjects, it is very normal to get stuck or want another opinion on your approach. I also joined the club’s Discord server, and through that I found even more useful resources such as job-hunting advice, internship tips, technical discussions, gaming channels, and other community spaces that made the club feel much more active and welcoming. What I liked most was that it did not feel like just a formal academic club. It felt like a real community where students with similar interests could learn from one another, ask for help, and also enjoy the social side of being around like-minded people. Overall, joining UCC felt like a very worthwhile step for me because it gave me access to a helpful network, useful resources, and future opportunities to learn, collaborate, and connect with others in cybersecurity.
