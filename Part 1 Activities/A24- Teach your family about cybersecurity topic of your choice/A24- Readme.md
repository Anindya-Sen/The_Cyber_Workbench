## A24- Teach your family about cybersecurity topic of your choice


For this activity, I chose to teach my parents about phishing attacks through social media because this is one of the most practical and relevant cybersecurity topics directly applicable to them. Since Bangladesh is still developing in terms of digital awareness, my parents were introduced to social media much later in life, so many of these online systems, page behaviours, advertisements, and links still feel unfamiliar to them. Because of that, I felt that they were more vulnerable to being misled by fake pages, suspicious advertisements, manipulated posts, and harmful links shared through comments or messages. To explain this properly, I prepared a short presentation titled “Not Everything on Social Media is Safe” and presented it to them on a Google Meet call. In the presentation, I started from the basics by explaining in very simple language what phishing means. I explained that phishing is when someone pretends to be trustworthy so that people click a link, open a message, give personal details, or download something harmful. I also explained that these attacks often work by creating curiosity, urgency, fear, or excitement, which pushes people into clicking too quickly without checking properly first. I then showed them the most common warning signs, such as a page looking official but having a strange name, poor profile details, suspicious links, or posts telling people to find the “full story” in the comments instead of on a trusted website. I also taught them that a blue tick badge alone is not enough to prove that a page is safe, and that they should look at the full picture before trusting a page or link.

During the discussion, I used examples that felt realistic to them. One of the examples I explained was the comment-link trap, where a page posts something shocking or interesting and tells people to click a link in the comments to get the full information. I told them that this is dangerous because the link may lead to a fake page, harmful website, or something malicious that could compromise their phone or account. I also told them not to treat social media as a reliable news source and instead cross-check important stories through proper news websites. During the presentation, my dad shared an experience of his own. He said that on Facebook he once saw an advertisement for the sale of a classical Bengali novel, which immediately caught his attention because he likes those books a lot. He said the page looked genuine and even had a blue tick badge, but at the same time the page had a strange name and no proper profile photo or cover photo, which made it look suspicious. He refreshed the page by mistake and lost it before interacting with it. I then explained to him that this is exactly why these scams are becoming harder to detect, because some pages can now look very polished and may even appear verified, but still show other warning signs. I told both of my parents that they should never trust a page just because it looks professional or has one feature that looks official. They should also avoid clicking on weird or unknown links even if the person or page looks familiar, because hacked or impersonated accounts can also spread phishing links.

I also added one personal story called “My Personal Experience of a Possible Trap” to make the lesson feel more real and relatable. In that part, I shared my own experience from just before coming to Australia, when I was trying to find a rental place in Perth. Since I did not know anyone there, I posted in a Facebook rental group and hoped for genuine responses. Instead, I noticed that many suspicious accounts replied with strange or random links and kept urging me to click on them. Some names looked obviously strange, while others looked normal but still felt untrustworthy, and the replies sounded too eager and too good to be true. Because I already had a little awareness about online risks, I chose not to engage with those links. During the presentation, I showed this example to my parents and explained that what looked like help could actually have been a phishing attempt or another malicious trap. I wanted them to understand that these things do not only happen to “other people” on the internet — they can happen in very normal situations, even when someone is simply trying to rent a room or solve an everyday problem.

I also used the presentation to teach them preventive steps they can take before any problem happens. I told them to turn on multi-factor authentication, make their accounts private, use strong and unique passwords, and avoid using profile or cover photos that reveal too much personal information. I also told them to keep their apps and phones updated, since updates often fix security weaknesses. One of the main things I wanted them to understand was that staying safe online is not about one single trick. It is about building good habits and checking things carefully before trusting them. I ended the presentation by reminding them of a very simple rule: if something online feels strange, rushed, or too good to be true, stop first and check it properly before clicking. Overall, this activity was very impactful because it was not just about explaining a cybersecurity concept in theory, but about helping my parents recognise how these attacks can appear in everyday life. Most importantly, it made them understand how easily people can be misled on social media, and learnt practical steps they can take early on to protect themselves and reduce the risk of being exploited.
