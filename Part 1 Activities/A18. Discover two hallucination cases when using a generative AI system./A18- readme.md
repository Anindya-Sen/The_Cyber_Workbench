# A18. Discover two hallucination cases when using a generative AI system.


One hallucination case I found especially useful was the Air Canada chatbot case, because it shows how an AI system can give false information in a situation that feels very real and serious. In this case, a passenger asked Air Canada’s chatbot about bereavement fares after his grandmother died. The chatbot told him he could buy a regular ticket first and then apply for a bereavement discount afterwards, but that information was wrong. When he later tried to claim the lower fare, Air Canada refused and relied on its written policy instead. Reporting on the case said Air Canada even argued that the chatbot was “responsible for its own actions,” which made the situation more controversial because the chatbot was still part of the company’s website and looked like an official source of help. The tribunal ruled in favour of the customer, and the airline had to pay compensation. What makes this such a strong hallucination example is that the chatbot did not just make up a harmless fact — it gave false policy advice in a moment when the user had a real reason to trust it, and that false answer directly affected money, travel, and a legal dispute. To me, this is one of the clearest examples of why hallucination in generative AI is dangerous: when the answer sounds confident and comes from an official-looking system, people may naturally rely on it even when it is wrong.

A more media-related hallucination case I found was the Chicago Sun-Times summer reading list incident in 2025, and the more I read about it, the more serious it seemed. A special summer section distributed with the paper included a reading list that recommended several books that simply did not exist, even though they were confidently attributed to real and well-known authors. Reporting explained that the content came through a third-party syndicated supplement, and later the content distributor, King Features, said the writer responsible had used AI and had not fact-checked the output. The Sun-Times later confirmed that AI-generated misinformation had appeared in the section and said it was reviewing its processes. What makes this case especially important is that the hallucination did not stay inside a private chat window. It became published media content that readers could easily assume had been researched and checked properly. Some of the fake titles looked believable enough because they matched real authors and sounded like realistic book names, which is exactly why AI hallucinations can be so risky in journalism and publishing. I think this is a strong example because it shows that hallucination is not only a problem for one careless user — once false AI content gets into a newspaper or public publication, it can mislead a large number of people and damage trust in the publication itself. It also shows that the danger is not always dramatic or technical. Sometimes the hallucination is simply polished, believable nonsense that slips through because nobody checked it carefully enough.
