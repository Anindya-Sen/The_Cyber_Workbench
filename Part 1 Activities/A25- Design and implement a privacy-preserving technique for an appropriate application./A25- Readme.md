## A25- Design and implement a privacy-preserving technique for an appropriate application.


One privacy problem that stood out to me is how easily people share screenshots from their laptops without realising how much private information may still be visible in them. This can happen in very normal situations such as sharing an email screenshot, a booking confirmation, a payment page, a student portal page, or even a simple chat screenshot while asking for help. At first, the user may think they are only sharing the main part of the image, but the screenshot may still contain names, email addresses, phone numbers, IDs, profile photos, booking numbers, QR codes, or private chat messages. I wanted to solve this problem because it feels very common, very realistic, and also very easy to miss in daily life. Instead of only writing about it in theory, I decided to design and implement a small AI-based privacy-preserving technique that could actually help someone check a screenshot before sharing it.

The technique I designed was a Privacy Screenshot Checker, which is a small custom GPT that reviews screenshots and identifies whether any private or sensitive information is still visible. The purpose of the tool is simple: before a screenshot is shared, the user can upload it to the GPT, and the GPT will check for possible privacy risks and tell the user what should be blurred, cropped, or removed. I designed it around a very practical privacy principle, which is data minimisation. Instead of letting private information be shared first and dealt with later, the technique helps reduce unnecessary exposure before the screenshot is sent anywhere. I set up the GPT with a clear role, a narrow task, and a structured response format. I made it check for things such as full names, email addresses, phone numbers, IDs, usernames, profile photos, booking numbers, QR codes, payment details, private messages, and even visible tab titles if they could reveal sensitive activity.

The implementation part was not just the idea itself, but the way I carefully built and improved the tool. I first created a preliminary instruction set for the GPT, where I told it to act as a screenshot privacy checker and return a privacy risk level, list the visible sensitive items, explain why they are risky, and recommend what to blur or crop. After testing that first version, I noticed that the GPT was sometimes too cautious and could over-flag harmless interface elements. Because of that, I improved the design by adding extra instructions and example cases, especially examples showing when blurring is not necessary. For example, I added guidance that normal battery icons, Wi-Fi icons, browser buttons, and harmless app icons should usually not be treated as privacy risks. I also included examples where a first name by itself may not always need blurring unless it is clearly tied to another identifying detail. This made the GPT more balanced and much more useful in practice.

I then tested the GPT on multiple screenshots to see whether it worked properly. I deliberately used both high-risk screenshots and low-risk screenshots so that I could see if the outputs were sensible. For the highly sensitive cases, I used screenshots that contained visible names, email addresses, account-related details, booking information, chat messages, or other private information. In these cases, the GPT correctly identified the risky elements, explained why they mattered, and recommended that the screenshot was not safe to share yet until those parts were hidden. For less sensitive cases, I used screenshots that only showed ordinary interface elements or harmless visual details, and the GPT was able to respond in a more measured way instead of flagging everything unnecessarily. I found these tests very useful because they showed that the outputs were not random. They were specific, practical, and generally credible. The GPT was able to distinguish between information that truly affected privacy and information that was only part of the normal interface.

What I found most valuable about this activity is that it showed me that a privacy-preserving technique does not always need to be a large software product or a very complex cryptographic system. It can also be a focused tool that solves a real privacy problem in an appropriate application. In this case, the application was screenshot sharing on a laptop, and the privacy-preserving technique was the use of an AI checker that helps users notice private information before it is exposed. I think this fits the task very well because I did not only identify the privacy issue, but also designed, implemented, tested, and improved a working solution for it. Overall, this activity felt meaningful because it was practical, easy to demonstrate with real examples, and directly connected to a problem that many people face in everyday digital life without even noticing it.
