## A03 – Discover security concepts used in your house

While thinking about security concepts used in my house, I realised that home security is not only about locking the main door. There are several physical security concepts used inside a house that help protect people and belongings in a more everyday way. For example, window grills and window locks act as another layer of protection besides the front entrance, because they make it harder for someone to enter through a weaker side of the house. Another concept is having an outside light or sensor light near the entrance, which improves visibility at night and reduces hidden areas around the house. I also noticed that keeping important belongings and documents inside drawers, cabinets, or specific private spaces is a simple form of internal protection, because not everything in a house is equally exposed. In addition, having a boundary grill gate before the main entrance is also a useful physical security concept because it creates an extra barrier before someone can even reach the door, and since that grill gate has its own lock, entry into the house is protected by two separate locks instead of just one. This adds an extra layer of security because even if someone reaches the first barrier, they still cannot directly access the main entrance. These examples showed me that household physical security often works through small layers that people may not always think about, but together they help make the home safer.

At the same time, my house also uses several digital security concepts in ways that are easy to overlook. One major example is the Wi-Fi router password, because that controls who can connect to the home network in the first place. Another is the use of screen locks and fingerprint locks on phones and laptops, which protect personal files and accounts if the device is lost or picked up by someone else at home. I also became more aware of privacy-based digital security through the settings on my phone. For example, when using the Amazon app on my iPhone, I always disable the option for Siri to learn from this app. Siri is a virtual assistant that learns from app activity to make suggestions, but I do not want my shopping-related information being used in ways that affect my privacy or lead to unnecessary targeted suggestions. In a similar way, I also turn off the “Allow Tracking” option on social media apps so that these apps cannot track my activity across other apps and services. For me, these are also important digital security concepts at home, because they are not about stopping a break-in, but about controlling how much of my personal data is collected, shared, and used in the background. This activity helped me understand that my house is both a physical and digital environment, and that home security now also includes privacy settings and data control on the devices I use every day.
